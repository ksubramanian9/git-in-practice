Exception Handling Safe deviations from the standard process

Exceptions are rare and temporary. This page explains when exceptions are justified, how to request them, and what controls are required while an exception is active.

When Exceptions Are Allowed

Must: Exceptions exist only to restore service, unblock security remediation, or keep a release train moving.

Must: Exceptions expire automatically with a date; renewals require a new approval.

Should: Prefer feature flags or configuration toggles over process exceptions whenever possible.

Must not: Grant exceptions for convenience (e.g., skipping tests to save time).

Request & Approval Workflow

Standard process
  1. Open a ticket labelled Exception with scope, risk, and duration.
  2. Attach the PR(s) involved and proposed mitigations (e.g., manual test plan).
  3. Obtain approval from the Engineering Manager and Platform within one business day.
  4. Communicate in the relevant channel; pin the exception status until closed.
Required safeguards
  • Explicit owner accountable for closing the exception.
  • Manual verification plan covering the skipped control.
  • Follow-up task to eliminate the root cause within 5 business days.
  • Retro once closed to prevent recurring exceptions.

Operating the Exception

Tracking: Keep a single dashboard of active exceptions with owners and expiry dates.

Visibility: Tag commits and releases created under an exception; note the compensating controls in release notes.

Closure: Exceptions automatically sunset on the expiry date; owners must confirm the control is restored and link evidence.

Audit: Platform reviews closed exceptions monthly to identify systemic fixes.

Rationale

Time-bound, visible exceptions keep us shipping during incidents without normalizing risky shortcuts. Clear ownership ensures the standard process is restored quickly.
